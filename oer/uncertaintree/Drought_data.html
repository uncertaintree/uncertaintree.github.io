1 Use for statistical teaching

Among others, this data can be used for basic plotting in an R course, but also to introduce linear regression on a simple applied example.

2 Introduction

Under this link here you can find a figure that looks ugly, but it fits it’s prupose.

A caption to it might read like this:

Adopted from Fischer et al. (2006), where labeled as Figure 2–5. Light blue and light green crosses and lines were added using R and TikZ. Lines between the added crosses are just a remainder from my workflow - it helps to be able to count through the points on the line when making small adjustments to the data-points - but should not make the impression that observations are time series or correlated in any other form.

As written, this figure is an adaption of Fischer et al. (2006) and shows data on tree growth (measured as basal area increment, BAI) in the year 2003 compared to the BAI in 2002 for pure (?) stands with two different tree species, Norway spruce (Picea abies) and common beech (Fagus sylvatica). Measurements were taken from several plot across the Alpine region.

We can describe the values on the y-axis more formally as: \[ y_i=\dfrac{\text{BAI}_{2003,i}}{\text{BAI}_{2002,i}} \] The x-axis shows elevation above sea-level in meters. In total, measurements for \(23\) for Norway spruce stands, and for \(11\) common beech stands were given, ie. \(i=1,\ldots,34\).

2.1 Data scratching workflow

There might exist specified R packages for scratching the observation values out of a scatter plot, but I just took the basic route – this might be higher effort, but it’s easily reproducible and results in what I need, a Figure I can include here – of making a screen shot, saved it as a .png-file, included it into a LaTeX document, and added values using TikZ.

3.1 A sligtly extended data source

The data published by Fischer et al. (2006) was also reused and republished in

Matthias Dobbertin, Markus Neumann and Hans-Werner Schroeck (2013): Chapter 10 - Tree Growth Measurements in Long-Term Forest Monitoring in Europe, DOI: DOI: 10.1016/B978-0-08-098222-9.00010-8.

I followed the same data scratching workflow there and found out that there were four additional observations for Norway spruce and two additional data-points for common beech. It is not mentioned or explained by the authors where these additional observations come from. Further, the regression line for Norway spruce doesn’t reproduce as perfectly as the one shown in the above figure, whereas the line for common beech does. So I just stick to the data we got from Fischer et al. (2006).
